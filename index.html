Is the patient's vision getting worse ("progressing")?

Visual field tests that detect a patient's sensitivity to light at 52 distinct points are a critical factor in detecting and determining the right treatment strategy for glaucoma.

Interpreting the results to determine if a patient's vision is, in fact, getting worse is challenging for 3 key reasons

  • Test Variation: Visual field tests depend on patient responses to flashes of light. Patient fatigue, lack of focus, or learning effects can cause variation in the results independent of any change in vision
  • Lack of a "gold standard" metric: Researchers have developed a range of metrics for assessing patient vision. Some take all 52 points into account (e.g., mean deviation), others take a cluster approach (e.g., AGIS, CIGTS), while others focus on trends at the individual point level (e.g., point-wise linear regression). Research has shown that these measures can often disagree leaving clinicians to make a judgement call between them
  • Too much paper: Today clinicians line up paper printouts of visual field results to compare results over time and make assessments on pateient progression

    A visual field is the core data structure for our product

    The visual field captures the results of patient's vision test at a particular visit in an eye-shaped matrix. The numbers represent a patient's sensitivity to light at 52-distinct points. Higher numbers represent stronger vision. There are three standard measures of vision on visual fields, which are included in our dataset:

  • Raw sensitivity: values of each tested point are listed in decibels in the sensitivity plot. Higher numbers mean the patient was able to see a more attenuated light, and thus has more sensitive vision at that location
  • Total deviation: numbers are the deviations in sensitivity from the expected values for a specific age. It is useful to compare with age-matched normal thresholds as sensitivity normally decreases gradually with age. Positive values represent areas of the field where the patient can see dimmer stimuli than the average individual of that age. Negative values represent decreased sensitivity from normal.
  • Pattern deviation: shows discrepancies within a patient's visual field by correcting for generalized decreases in visual sensitivity. It is useful to show localized areas of sensitivity loss hidden within a field that is diffusely depressed.
